Knowledge Compression

According to Ted Chiang (2022), “the best way to devise a way to efficiently compress a set of data is to understand them.”

This type of understanding, for humans, is “formulaic,” whereas for machines, it’s “probabilistic.” It’s not certain which is more efficient. However, it should first be acknowledged that probabilistic methods are indeed a way of understanding. As for efficiency, specifically in terms of human understanding, it usually requires low complexity (it gives the simplest explanation of data) but potentially high logical depth (it explains a lot and may require a very long time to compute all its implications), or the size of information entropy as defined by Shannon. Take the Fibonacci sequence as an example: humans can describe it through a refined formula; but as for how machines understand it, whether a “probabilistic” description is longer or shorter than a “formulaic” one, is currently indeterminable.

Different information poses different levels of difficulty for humans and machines. For humans: Information with Fibonacci-like patterns is naturally suitable for compression; information that seems like gibberish and hard to patternize is naturally unsuitable for compression. This is because “formulaic” methods are the most effective ways humans have found to describe and communicate Fibonacci-like information, representing a path dependence in human information expression. For machines: Fibonacci sequences and gibberish may be considered equally, with even the possibility that gibberish is easier to compress. Probability is a path dependence in how machines express information, and machines might find a more efficient way through “probabilistic” methods, a way that is not understood by humans. Moreover, the advantage of probabilistic methods is that they unify the form of understanding all knowledge.
