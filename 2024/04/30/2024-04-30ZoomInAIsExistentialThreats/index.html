Zoom In AIs Existential Threats

Geoffrey Hinton left Google last year, seeking for “speaking freely about the risks of AI”. In his free lecture at LSE this March [1], he voiced concerns about AI’s long-term existential threats. He argued that AI, through the sharing of weights or gradients, achieves a level of efficiency in digital computation that exceeds human biological computation. Furthermore, he deemed human’s “sentience defense” as untenable, equating it to the errors of “religious fundamentalism”, based on his belief that subjective experience is not uniquely human.
Acknowledging that the sentience defense is untenable, we must ask whether this truly spells an existential crisis for humanity. It’s not enough to dwell on this seemingly grim assertion; instead, we should delve deeper into its implications. The issue of resource plundering comes to my mind as an immediate concern, but this presupposes that AI would have survival instincts akin to humans and vie for identical resources. Currently, humans and AI appear to be in competition for resources, yet it’s debatable whether an advanced AI, if motivated by survival, would persist in such an exhaustive and unsustainable manner. Wouldn’t it instead evolve towards greater efficiency?
To ascertain if my definition of an existential crisis is biased, I turned to GPT-4 for insight, and here is my feedback around its key points.

Firstly, it raised concerns about unemployment (in terms of replacing human labour) and social structure, as well as personal relationships (in terms of mimicking human emotion) and social interaction, which I’ve categorised as physical and psychological concerns, respectively. While both dimensions are crucial, they don’t strike at the core of an existential crisis, so I won’t delve into them here.
Secondly, there’s the issue of ethics and control, such as defining AI’s ethical and legislative status—a debate akin to cloning. I believe if technological advancement maintains a manageable pace, we’ll have ample time to reach a considered consensus. Hence, this should be viewed as an opportunity for self-reflection, not a threat.
Thirdly, loss of human autonomy. While AI decision-making raises issues such as policy-related explainability and interpretability, it does not necessarily lead to the loss of human autonomy as a whole. In my earlier reflections on critical thinking[2], I considered this faculty is overrated, and I see no reason why this skill cannot be “offloaded” to AI—hat tip to Daniel Dennett for an apt term again. Consequently, autonomy is not central to the existential crisis.
Finally, the question of creativity. For now, I regard creativity as a distinctly human faculty, which makes it the only issue directly related to the existential crisis. Will AI evolve to possess creativity? I lack a definitive answer, but an interesting trend emerged from my preliminary interviews: individuals with liberal arts background seem more assured of human’s creativity and originality compared to those from STEM fields.
